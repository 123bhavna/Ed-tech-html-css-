Introduction to Financial Statements

What are Financial statements?

Financial statements are comprehensive summaries of a company's financial, operational, and investing activities, offering a snapshot of its economic performance and financial position. Common components include the balance sheet, income statement, cash flow statement, and statement of shareholders' equity. These statements play a pivotal role in conveying essential financial details to stakeholders, aiding decision-making and performance assessment.

Users of financial statements

1.Investors:

1. Use financial statements to assess a company's risk and potential return on investment.

2. Creditors:

1. Rely on financial statements to evaluate a company's capacity to repay its debts.

3. Management:

1. Utilizes financial statements to monitor the company's progress and identify areas for improvement.

4. Government:

1. Government agencies leverage financial statements for regulatory purposes, safeguarding businesses and investors.

5. Employees:

1. Gain insights into job security and the company's ability to provide benefits and compensation.

Types of financial statements

1. Balance sheet -

A balance sheet is a financial statement that provides a snapshot of a company's financial position at a specific point in time. It showcases the company's assets, liabilities, and shareholders' equity, offering a comprehensive view of its financial health.

Components of Balance sheet

1.Assets

Current Assets: Items expected to be converted into cash or used up within one year (e.g., cash, accounts receivable, inventory). Non-Current Assets: Long-term assets with a useful life beyond one year (e.g., property, plant, equipment, intangible assets).

2. Liabilities

a. Current Liabilities: Obligations due within one year (e.g., accounts payable, short-term debt).
b. Example:Non-Current Liabilities: Long-term obligations extending beyond one year (e.g., long-term debt, deferred tax liabilities).

3. Shareholders' Equity:

a. Common Stock: Represents ownership in the company through shares.
b.Retained Earnings: Accumulated profits retained for business growth.

2. Income Statement -

The income statement, also known as a profit and loss statement, is a financial document that provides a summary of a company's revenues, expenses, and profits over a specific period, typically a fiscal quarter or year. It is one of the key financial statements used to assess a company's financial performance.

Components of income statement

Revenues (Sales): Represents the total income generated from the sale of goods or services.
2. Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): Accounts for the direct costs associated with producing goods or delivering services.
3.Gross Profit: Calculated by subtracting COGS from total revenues, indicating the profit after accounting for the direct costs.
4.Operating Expenses: Includes various costs incurred in the regular operations of the business, such as salaries, rent, utilities, and marketing expenses.
5.Operating Income (Operating Profit): Obtained by subtracting operating expenses from the gross profit, reflecting the profit from core business operations.
6. Other Income and Expenses: Includes non-operating items such as interest income, interest expenses, and other gains or losses.
7. Net Income Before Tax: The sum of operating income and other income/expenses, representing the company's profit before taxes.
8. Income Tax Expense: Accounts for the company's tax obligations based on its taxable income.
9. Net Income (Net Profit or Net Loss): The final figure obtained by subtracting income tax expense from net income before tax. This represents the company's overall profit or loss.

3. Cashflow Statement:

A cash flow statement is a financial statement summarizing how changes in balance sheet accounts and income affect cash and cash equivalents. It categorizes cash inflows and outflows into three main activities: operating, investing, and financing activities.

Components of cashflow statement

A cash flow statement summarizes how changes in balance sheet accounts and income affect cash and cash equivalents. It categorizes cash inflows and outflows into three main activities: operating, investing, and financing activities.

Components of a Cash Flow Statement:

1.Operating Activities:

Operating activities represent the day-to-day business operations that generate revenue.

1.Cash Inflows: Receipts from sales, interest, and dividends.
2.Cash Outflows: Payments to suppliers, employees, and operating expenses.

2. Investing Activities:

Investing activities involve transactions in the purchase and sale of long-term assets.

1.Cash Inflows: Proceeds from asset sales and investments.
2.Cash Outflows: Payments for new assets and investments.

3. Financing Activities:

Financing activities on a company's cash flow statement involve transactions with the company's owners (shareholders) and creditors.

Investing activities involve transactions in the purchase and sale of long-term assets.

1.Cash Inflows: Proceeds from borrowing or issuing stock.
2.Cash Outflows: Debt repayments and dividends to shareholders.
